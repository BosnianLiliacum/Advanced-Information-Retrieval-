  Comment: You buy a Synology box, you stick hard drives in it, you download the apps on your phone. Synology is extremely simple. Anything you try to build yourself is going to be more complex than a Synology NAS.

If you just want a hard drive plugged into your router that's cool, but what are you going to do when that hard drive fails and your whole family photos collection disappears? Better buy a second hard drive just in case then. But what happens if there is a fire or other natural disaster and both your drives get destroyed?

You might think I'm being pedantic here, but this stuff happens. All the time. Even iCloud is going to have disks that fail because disks fail. There is no getting around that. The solution to this is redundancy (raid) and off-site backups. This stuff is built into a Synology NAS by default.

If you go with a JBOD system (just a bunch of disks) you better think of the future and what you're going to do when something fails.

Comment 4:
  Author: Vogete
  Comment: The market for this kind of thing is really small. You basically want a product that requires zero technical expertise, it just works, all the time, but the customer wants to make sure that the bytes are physically in the house.

Sounds like a reasonable product at first until you consider a few things. Most non-technical people use the cloud because "it just works", all the time, every day. Now, as soon you move it away from the cloud, the following issues need to be solved for non-technical customers:

- data backup, redundancy, etc: if the NAS is knocked on the floor, got water damage, burned the house down, how can the customer not lose everything?
- storage drives die over time. Someone needs to maintain them. Who, and how, and for what cost?
- do non-technical people even have an Ethernet cable for that NAS? Is it gonna support WiFi then? 
- how do they access it when they are not home? Is there a reverse proxy service customers will have to pay for, to ensure internet access? If so, how much will it be, and how is it gonna be better for the customer, knowing the data still passes through some servers? Depending on the cost, why would customers pay for that instead of icloud?
- this ugly (or pretty) box is now sitting in the apartment/house, emitting heat, maybe noise, consuming power

There's just too many variables in this for non-technical people to be worth it.
